I'm an operator & builder helping growth-stage companies turn data and narratives into decisions, faster.

The best-run organizations I've seen share one trait: everyone maintains a clear, shared picture of reality — what's decided, what's true, and who owns what. When that's true, decisions get made faster and stay made. Priorities don't need to be re-litigated. The gap between what was agreed in the room and what actually happened closes. McKinsey's research puts a number on it: companies with strong alignment deliver three times the shareholder returns of those without it.

Most organizations aren't there. Harvard Business Review found that executives report 82% strategic alignment within their companies — but when researchers measured actual alignment, it was closer to 23%. The gap is invisible from inside. Goals multiply without hierarchy. Meetings generate discussion but not decisions anyone acts on. Ownership becomes implicit. The organization keeps functioning — but on informal memory and repeated clarification rather than a shared picture of reality.

Five tools. One system.

Most organizations have systems for capturing information — almost none for turning it into coherent judgment. The result is predictable: metrics live in dashboards, decisions disappear into meeting notes, pipeline risk hides inside CRM reports, strategic drift accumulates quietly inside roadmaps.

The problem isn't information. The problem is synthesis.

The first four tools collect and interpret signals from different parts of the organization. The fifth synthesizes them into a single view of reality. Together they form an early prototype of what I believe is missing from the modern software stack: infrastructure for turning information into durable judgment.

01
WBR Generator
What happened?
Live

Most Weekly Business Reviews are rituals, not diagnostics. The WBR Generator deliberately separates two layers: a deterministic Pandas pipeline computes what actually happened — week-over-week changes, 4-week trend slopes, derived ratios, and z-score anomaly detection against historical baseline. The reasoning layer (Claude Sonnet) receives only structured facts — never raw numbers — and is explicitly instructed not to summarize, not to hallucinate absent fields, and to produce decisions required this week and non-obvious leadership questions. A DATA AVAILABILITY block prevents hallucination on fields that aren't in the CSV.

"The bottleneck isn't data — it's interpretation. Dashboards can't solve a judgment problem."

02
Meeting Intelligence
What was decided?
Live

The gap between what got decided in a meeting and what anyone acts on is where execution debt is born. Meeting Intelligence runs deterministic preprocessing before Claude sees anything — timestamp stripping, speaker normalization, commitment detection, decision signal scoring. Then a two-pass Claude API call using structured tool use: Pass 1 extracts decisions, open items, blockers, and unresolved questions with confidence scoring; Pass 2 drafts a follow-up from structured output only — raw transcripts never reach the follow-up pass. Name a recurring series and the tool tracks patterns across sessions via Supabase, surfacing what keeps getting left unresolved week over week.

"Anyone can summarize a meeting. The judgment is in knowing what a decision actually looks like versus a discussion."

03
Pipeline & Forecast Synthesizer
What will happen?
Live

CRMs capture activity. They don't capture judgment. The Pipeline Synthesizer connects directly to HubSpot via REST API — resolving owner IDs to rep names and internal stage IDs to human-readable labels — then runs deterministic preprocessing before Claude sees anything: concentration analysis, slippage detection, deal age, days since last activity, weighted pipeline by stage probability. Claude then reasons like a CRO preparing for a board meeting, producing six structured sections via forced tool use: executive summary, forecast confidence, revenue risks, revenue opportunities, rep insights, and leadership actions.

"3.2x coverage is a signal. 'The forecast risk is concentration, not volume' is judgment. This tool produces the latter."

04
Initiative Intelligence
What is getting done?
In Development

Most roadmaps don't fail from a lack of ideas — they fail because no one notices when the work and the strategy drift apart. Initiative Intelligence ingests a CSV export from any project management tool (Linear, Jira, Asana, Notion) and produces three layered outputs: a deterministic portfolio snapshot of what exists and who owns it; an execution risk surface identifying blocked, stalled, and single-owner dependencies; and a strategic drift analysis — the centerpiece — that maps active work against the stated quarterly strategy and surfaces the delta. What percentage of effort connects to the stated priority? What's consuming resources with no visible strategic connection? What's missing entirely?

"Organizations don't suffer from a shortage of initiatives. They suffer from an inability to distinguish motion from strategic progress."

05
Executive Attention Synthesizer
What matters now?
In Development

The first four tools each process one organizational domain into structured judgment. The Executive Attention Synthesizer reads across all four simultaneously — metrics signal, decision signal, revenue signal, execution signal — and surfaces the patterns no individual system can see. Three signals in isolation are manageable. Four signals pointing at the same underlying problem from different directions is a diagnosis. The output answers the three questions a leadership team actually needs answered: what requires attention now, what is consuming attention unnecessarily, and what decision cannot be postponed.

"Every system tells a different story. The job isn't collecting more stories. It's finding the one they're all trying to tell."

The Argument

Why organizations accumulate execution debt despite having more information than ever

Over the last two decades, companies built extraordinary infrastructure for capturing information — dashboards, CRMs, project tools, meeting transcripts, knowledge bases. The assumption was that access to data was the binding constraint. It wasn't.

"The real constraint is what happens after information appears: how it is interpreted, turned into decisions, assigned ownership, and kept aligned as reality changes. On that front, we built almost nothing."

Why? Because decision infrastructure is hard to sell. The value of a CRM is legible: here are your contacts, your pipeline, your history. The value of a system that maintains organizational decision state is diffuse, slow to show up in metrics, and politically uncomfortable. Genuine clarity exposes misalignment and accountability gaps that organizations have strong incentives to keep ambiguous. So the market built what was measurable and avoided what was necessary.

The failure mode is predictable. Goals multiply without hierarchy. Metrics proliferate but lose trust. Meetings generate discussion but not decisions anyone acts on. Ownership becomes implicit. Companies stop running on a shared picture of reality and start running on informal memory and repeated clarification — still functioning, but no longer synchronized.

AI makes this gap decisive. As the cost of generating outputs collapses, the bottleneck shifts from production to coherence. More analysis, more content, more code — producing entropy at higher velocity without a layer to hold it together.

Organizations have systems of record for data, communication, and tasks. For direction, state, and accountability — the three things that determine whether work actually happens — they have fragments. Static, decentralized, and out of sync the moment reality moves. That's what I'm building.
