I'm an operator & builder helping growth-stage companies turn data and narratives into decisions, faster.

The best-run organizations I've seen share one trait: everyone maintains a clear, shared picture of reality — what's decided, what's true, and who owns what. When that's true, decisions get made faster and stay made. Priorities don't need to be re-litigated. The gap between what was agreed in the room and what actually happened closes. McKinsey's research puts a number on it: companies with strong alignment deliver three times the shareholder returns of those without it.

Most organizations aren't there. Harvard Business Review found that executives report 82% strategic alignment within their companies — but when researchers measured actual alignment, it was closer to 23%. The gap is invisible from inside. Goals multiply without hierarchy. Meetings generate discussion but not decisions anyone acts on. Ownership becomes implicit. The organization keeps functioning — but on informal memory and repeated clarification rather than a shared picture of reality.

Five tools. One system.

Most organizations have systems for capturing information — almost none for turning it into coherent judgment. The result is predictable: metrics live in dashboards, decisions disappear into meeting notes, pipeline risk hides inside CRM reports, strategic drift accumulates quietly inside roadmaps.

The problem isn't information. The problem is synthesis.

The first four tools collect and interpret signals from different parts of the organization. The fifth synthesizes them into a single view of reality. Together they form an early prototype of what I believe is missing from the modern software stack: infrastructure for turning information into durable judgment.

The Argument

Why organizations accumulate execution debt despite having more information than ever

Over the last two decades, companies built extraordinary infrastructure for capturing information — dashboards, CRMs, project tools, meeting transcripts, knowledge bases. The assumption was that access to data was the binding constraint. It wasn't.

"The real constraint is what happens after information appears: how it is interpreted, turned into decisions, assigned ownership, and kept aligned as reality changes. On that front, we built almost nothing."

Why? Because decision infrastructure is hard to sell. The value of a CRM is legible: here are your contacts, your pipeline, your history. The value of a system that maintains organizational decision state is diffuse, slow to show up in metrics, and politically uncomfortable. Genuine clarity exposes misalignment and accountability gaps that organizations have strong incentives to keep ambiguous. So the market built what was measurable and avoided what was necessary.

The failure mode is predictable. Goals multiply without hierarchy. Metrics proliferate but lose trust. Meetings generate discussion but not decisions anyone acts on. Ownership becomes implicit. Companies stop running on a shared picture of reality and start running on informal memory and repeated clarification — still functioning, but no longer synchronized.

AI makes this gap decisive. As the cost of generating outputs collapses, the bottleneck shifts from production to coherence. More analysis, more content, more code — producing entropy at higher velocity without a layer to hold it together.

Organizations have systems of record for data, communication, and tasks. For direction, state, and accountability — the three things that determine whether work actually happens — they have fragments. Static, decentralized, and out of sync the moment reality moves. That's what I'm building.
